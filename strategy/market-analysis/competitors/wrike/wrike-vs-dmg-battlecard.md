# Wrike vs DMG — Mini Battlecard

**Snapshot date**: 2026-04-27
**Scope**: Positioning for conversations where Wrike appears as a strong workflow reference.

## Core thesis

Wrike automates operational execution (intake validation, triage, assignment, compliance workflows).
DMG focuses on strategic arbitration: formalizing how conflicting signals are weighed, decided, justified, and historized.

## One-line positioning

Wrike helps teams run work.
DMG helps organizations decide which work should win when priorities conflict.

## Where Wrike is strong

- Strong workflow and project operations layer.
- Structured intake and request quality gates.
- Automated triage and assignment under predefined rules.
- Enterprise governance controls for operations (permissions, auditability, testing).
- Broad market validation through large-scale customers.

## Where Wrike remains limited for DMG's wedge

- Limited explicit model for strategic arbitration across conflicting priorities.
- Weak formalization of decision rationale at cross-team governance level.
- Limited decision-memory layer for longitudinal comparison of arbitration quality.
- Operational governance does not equal strategic governance.

## DMG attack lines

- "Visibility is not arbitration."
- "Structured requests are not formalized strategic decisions."
- "Automated assignment is not cross-stakeholder priority governance."
- "A dashboard can show delays; it does not explain why one trade-off is legitimate."

## DMG defense lines

- DMG is complementary to workflow stacks when needed; replacement is not required.
- DMG starts where workflow tools usually stop: conflict resolution between competing priorities.
- DMG creates explicit, auditable decision rationale that can be reused.
- DMG reduces decision entropy by making arbitration rules visible and discussable.

## Objections and responses

- **Objection**: "Wrike already does governance."
  **Response**: Wrike provides operational governance and control. DMG addresses strategic arbitration between conflicting priorities and captures decision rationale.

- **Objection**: "Existing real-time dashboards are already in place."
  **Response**: Real-time status answers "what is happening". DMG answers "what should win now, why, and with which trace".

- **Objection**: "Another layer will add complexity."
  **Response**: DMG can be scoped to one arbitration flow with a limited pilot, without replacing current systems.

- **Objection**: "Managers can decide without a dedicated tool."
  **Response**: Individual decisions exist, but formalized and historized collective arbitration is usually fragmented and hard to reuse.

## Discovery question to anchor every call

"Maintenant que vous voyez toutes ces données temps réel, comment décidez-vous concrètement les top-3 priorités ?"

## Fast qualification checklist

- Is there a recurring conflict between valid priorities?
- Is arbitration rationale explicit, documented, and reusable?
- Is decision history searchable and comparable across similar cases?
- Can stakeholders challenge and refine decision rules transparently?

If at least two answers are "no", DMG's wedge is likely active.
