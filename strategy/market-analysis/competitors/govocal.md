# Go Vocal

**Snapshot date**: 2026-04-16

## Why this file matters

Go Vocal matters because it is a serious commercial participation platform rather than a mostly civic-cause-centered consultation brand.

It is useful as a second anchor next to Make.org because it makes the category less dependent on one example.

Older records, legacy links, and public procurement traces may still refer to the company as CitizenLab.

## Current verified public signals

From Go Vocal public material, visible signals include:

- positioning as a community engagement platform trusted by 600 governments
- a broad toolbox covering surveys, voting, ideation, mapping, proposals, participatory budgeting, and citizens' assemblies
- explicit workflow language around engaging residents, streamlining workflows, and turning input into insights
- AI-assisted analysis through its Sensemaking tooling
- a founder-led SaaS company identity and B Corp positioning

Public impact signals currently shown include:

- 600+ governments and organizations across 20 countries
- 25,000+ projects launched
- 1,500,000+ community members engaged on the platform

Public case studies also show concrete scale in areas such as:

- participatory budgeting
- planning and placemaking
- community proposals
- comprehensive planning
- climate and public-safety engagement

## What the product appears to be

Go Vocal looks like a full-stack community-engagement SaaS platform with some service support.

Its public product language centers on three layers:

- Engage: multiple participation methods across the participation ladder
- Configure and manage: workflow and project administration
- Decide: AI-assisted analysis, dashboards, and reporting

The key product modules publicly visible include:

- surveys
- polls
- voting and prioritization
- ideation
- mapping
- community proposals
- participatory budgeting
- citizens' assemblies
- tools that digitize offline input into the online workflow

This is important because it shows a more modular and productized offer than Make.org's public consultation-plus-services presentation.

## How Go Vocal gets to faster analysis

Its public product language is fairly explicit here.

The current mechanism appears to be a combination of:

- AI Sensemaking that organizes and summarizes large amounts of textual and quantitative input
- dashboards that track participation, sentiment, and representativeness in real time
- a report builder that compresses stakeholder reporting work
- tools that convert paper forms and other offline feedback into usable digital inputs with less manual re-entry

The public claim is not that uncertainty disappears.

It is that analysis and reporting become faster and less labor-intensive.

That is strategically revealing.

It suggests the underlying problem is still assumed to exist, and the product promise is to process it more efficiently.

That is a meaningful but narrower claim than DMG's thesis.

The newer OECD write-up makes that public claim more concrete.

In the Cambridge case it describes Go Vocal's AI sensemaking tooling as helping the council group participant opinions automatically and rank them in priority order so their contributions could be analyzed, with a reported 50 percent reduction in estimated manual processing time.

That matters for two reasons.

First, it is explicit evidence that buyers value reduction of execution pain in the analysis workflow itself.

Second, the wording suggests that Go Vocal's value partly comes from assigning structure after the fact so noisy participation can be converted into something more sortable and more actionable.

That is still a serious capability, but it is different from DMG's stronger thesis that influence and tradeoffs should be structured earlier so the signal is more decision-usable before downstream interpretation begins.

## How Go Vocal structures participation

Go Vocal does structure participation.

It does not just open a blank text box and wait.

Its public model is built around a participation ladder with progressively different formats:

- Inform
- Consult
- Involve
- Collaborate
- Empower

Inside that ladder, it offers specific structured methods such as:

- surveys and polls
- voting and prioritization
- option analysis
- document annotation
- ideation and mapping
- community proposals
- citizens' assemblies
- participatory budgeting

So the correct contrast with DMG is not:

- Go Vocal does no structuring
- DMG does structuring

The better contrast is:

- Go Vocal structures channels and collection formats so institutions can gather and analyze participation more effectively
- DMG aims to structure how influence is earned, constrained, and spent so the resulting behavior is more decision-grade under real execution limits

## Why it matters for DMG

Go Vocal is relevant when the buyer needs:

- one central participation platform instead of fragmented engagement tools
- more representative participation than town halls or scattered surveys provide
- faster analysis of qualitative and quantitative input
- a reusable operating system for public participation workflows

That overlaps with DMG on:

- structured participation
- demand visibility
- input analysis

## Where it still differs from DMG

Go Vocal remains closer to:

- public-sector and civic engagement infrastructure
- broad input collection across many methods
- reporting and synthesis for planners, communicators, and public teams
- analysis tooling that helps institutions understand community priorities

DMG is still stronger when the main problem is:

- arbitration under scarcity
- recurring prioritization pressure
- shaping decision-grade behavior rather than only collecting broader input
- combining participation with explicit execution constraints

The clearest reason is that Go Vocal still appears largely oriented toward surfacing what residents prefer across structured formats.

That is useful, but it does not by itself answer a harder operational question:

- when scarce budget, time, staff capacity, or implementation slots force repeated tradeoffs, which signals should carry more decision weight, under what rules, and why

That is where DMG's weighting and governance hypothesis becomes more relevant.

One plausible DMG service layer follows directly from that gap.

DMG would not only provide infrastructure.

It could also help a tenant decide which rules should determine influence in the first place, based on the tenant's real operating constraints, business goals, and decision risks.

The difference is not simply that DMG is less democratic.

The stronger framing is that DMG can make influence rules more explicit instead of quietly letting loudness, availability, or raw activity become the default weighting system.

So the contrast is similar to Make.org, but with a different operating model.

Make.org feels more consultation-and-service heavy.

Go Vocal feels more workflow-platform-and-toolbox heavy.

## Why this case is strategically useful

Go Vocal sharpens two important lessons.

First, there is clearly a market for productized participation infrastructure, not only bespoke consultation projects.

Second, even the more productized players still compete heavily on turning large amounts of participation into understandable insight.

That means the category signal remains real:

- organizations already buy software to structure participation
- organizations already buy analysis tooling to make sense of large-scale input

The broader strategic question for DMG is whether it can claim a stronger position by making interaction rules themselves more economically meaningful than generic engagement toolkits do.

If the answer is yes, the thesis becomes:

- Go Vocal helps institutions hear more people and understand them faster
- DMG aims to make participation outputs more operationally weighted, so expressed preference is not only broader but more decision-usable

## Cost and operating-model implication

Compared with Make.org, Go Vocal appears more scalable and productized.

Compared with DMG, it still appears oriented toward broad participation and downstream synthesis more than scarce-influence governance.

Its public AI language is also revealing.

It sells faster understanding of large amounts of input, including text-heavy community feedback.

That supports the broader category insight that many serious competitors still create value after the interaction through organization, summarization, and reporting.

## Local spending traces worth following

The local spending data you surfaced is useful because it gives a concrete clue on order size.

Inside the January 2026 Tewkesbury Borough Council spending file, there are repeat entries for Go Vocal under the Joint Core Strategy budget line, including 14,500 pounds and 9,917 pounds entries that appear twice in the same month.

That should still be treated carefully until the council accounting context is fully understood.

The duplicate entries could reflect staged posting, internal transfer logic, or repeated charges rather than four distinct purchases.

Even so, it is a useful signal that Go Vocal can sit inside meaningful subscription-level budget lines for a district council rather than only inside tiny experimental spend.

Tewkesbury itself is a non-metropolitan district council in Gloucestershire with a population of roughly 101,949, which makes the order size more useful as a real procurement clue.

The Mid Suffolk spending file adds a second council signal.

There, a June 2025 line shows `GoVocal NV` receiving `2,250 pounds` under `Strategic Policy, Performance and Insight`, classified as `Contracted Services`.

Mid Suffolk is also a non-metropolitan district council, with a population of roughly 110,775.

That matters because it suggests Go Vocal can appear in more than one budget shape:

- higher recurring subscription-style lines tied to strategic planning or platform use
- smaller contracted-services or policy-support lines attached to a narrower operational need

The shape of these clues also supports a broader reading.

They are not just tiny experimental payments.

They look compatible with a productized platform sold with recurring service or support layers, which fits the broader procurement-friendly picture.

That broader reading also matches council-owned presentation pages.

Those pages do not provide contract value, but they do reinforce the idea that Go Vocal is often adopted as a recurring institutional consultation surface rather than a one-off experiment.

## French ecosystem and sovereignty signal

The French civic-tech picture adds a second lesson that is not mainly about pricing.

In April 2026, the Decidim Association publicly stated that Open Source Politics had been acquired by Go Vocal after financial difficulties over several years.

That matters because Open Source Politics was one of the main French Decidim service providers and had built live petition infrastructure for both the French Senate and the French National Assembly.

So the strategic issue is not only who wins a software subscription.

It is also who controls the service layer and continuity layer around democratic infrastructure once important institutions depend on it.

For DMG, that is useful because it sharpens a broader point investors and institutional buyers both care about:

- infrastructure value increases when the system becomes embedded
- but embedded infrastructure also raises sovereignty, continuity, and dependency questions

That does not make Go Vocal weaker as a company.

It does show that closed infrastructure can generate a different risk profile once public institutions depend on it for politically sensitive participation workflows.

That is especially relevant in France, where the public-code and technological-sovereignty argument is more politically legible than in some purely commercial markets.

## Method and trust caveat

Public consultation platforms also surface a second kind of risk.

The political problem is not only software cost.

It is whether the process is seen as representative, sincere, and decision-relevant.

The Alsace consultation controversy is useful here because the criticism did not focus only on participation numbers.

It also focused on duplicate voting, lack of strong identity control, representativeness, campaign cost, and whether the process could actually produce decisions rather than mainly symbolic pressure.

That matters for DMG because it reinforces a broader rule.

Participation infrastructure is judged not only by collection volume or analysis speed, but also by whether the rules of the process make the output feel governable and credible.

This is still not enough to reconstruct a full pricing model.

But it is useful evidence that councils of similar scale may accept both platform-style and service-style spend around the same vendor.
