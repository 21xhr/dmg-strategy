# Decidim

**Snapshot date**: 2026-04-16

## Why this file matters

Decidim matters because it is one of the clearest examples of participation infrastructure being treated as democratic architecture rather than only as a hosted consultation service.

It is the strongest third anchor in this category because it clarifies the difference between:

- proprietary consultation or engagement vendors
- open democratic infrastructure with configurable participation rules

That makes it strategically useful for DMG even when the product overlap is not exact.

## Current verified public signals

From public Decidim material, visible signals include:

- positioning as a digital platform for citizen participation
- explicit support for public institutions, organizations, and collectives of different sizes
- free/libre open-source positioning with a strong democratic-governance narrative
- broad use by cities, public institutions, and national or supranational bodies
- a live governance community around the product itself through Metadecidim

Publicly named users and instances include examples such as:

- Barcelona
- the European Commission citizens' engagement platform
- NYC
- Helsinki
- the French Senate
- the French National Assembly
- Geneva
- Brasil Participativo

This matters because it shows Decidim is not a small niche civic-tech experiment.

It is real democratic infrastructure used in serious institutional contexts.

## Concrete French institutional examples

The French Senate and French National Assembly examples should be read carefully.

The currently visible public implementations are not the chambers' internal legislative voting systems.

They are public petition platforms built with Decidim.

For the National Assembly, the platform explicitly allows citizens to submit petitions and sign existing ones, with some petitions eligible for discussion in standing committees and, under certain conditions, in public session.

For the Senate, the public platform explains that petitions reaching 100,000 signatures within 6 months are transmitted to the Conference of Presidents, which may decide to give them follow-up.

So the concrete lesson is not that Decidim powers parliamentary roll-call voting.

It is that Decidim can power structured citizen-to-institution intake of public demands in serious national settings.

## What the product appears to be

Decidim looks like a configurable participation operating system.

Its public model is built around:

- spaces for participation such as processes, assemblies, votings, and initiatives
- components such as proposals, voting, results, accountability, meetings, surveys, comments, newsletters, and participatory texts
- modules that extend the platform further

The strongest product idea is not only participation collection.

It is configurable participatory architecture.

Publicly, Decidim emphasizes:

- phased participatory processes
- multiple participation spaces
- formal traceability from proposals to results to accountability
- business rules that can be used to solve different participatory needs

That makes it closer to infrastructure than to a one-off consultation campaign.

## Why it matters for DMG

Decidim is relevant because it proves that some buyers are willing to adopt systems that require architectural choices, governance design, and more explicit participation structures.

That overlaps with DMG on:

- participation architecture
- governance rule design
- configurable process design
- structured progression from input to decision or follow-up

This is important because it supports one of your stronger intuitions: forcing structured interaction is not alien to the market.

Serious organizations already accept this logic when the infrastructure is valuable enough.

## Where Decidim still differs from DMG

Decidim remains closer to:

- civic and institutional participation infrastructure
- configurable democratic process design
- transparency, traceability, and accountability of participatory processes
- broad participation spaces for consultation, assemblies, voting, and follow-up

DMG is still stronger when the core problem is:

- recurring arbitration under scarcity
- explicit prioritization pressure inside ongoing operations
- economically meaningful tradeoffs between competing demands
- governance logic that sits closer to execution constraints than to public participation workflows

That said, scarcity still matters in these environments.

The scarcity is just not always expressed as budget allocation alone.

In civic and parliamentary environments it often appears as:

- limited agenda space
- limited committee attention
- limited hearing and deliberation capacity
- limited administrative follow-up capacity
- limited legitimacy for ignoring visible demand

That is one reason Decidim still matters for DMG market analysis.

It shows that structured participation tooling is valuable even where response capacity is constrained in political or institutional ways rather than only in operational-budget terms.

So Decidim is not a direct clone.

But it is a strong proof that structured participatory architecture is a real category, not only a theoretical possibility.

## Why Decidim is strategically different from Make.org and Go Vocal

Make.org is closer to consultation-plus-services and post-hoc synthesis.

Go Vocal is closer to a productized engagement SaaS with analysis tooling.

Decidim is closer to open democratic infrastructure and configurable process architecture.

That makes Decidim especially useful for DMG because it weakens the objection that forcing structure is too heavy or too unnatural.

The market already contains serious actors whose value partly comes from making participatory structure explicit.

This truth is still environment-bounded.

It proves tolerance for structured participation inside public, civic, and institutional settings.

It does not automatically prove that every commercial environment will accept the same amount of procedural weight.

In this context, tolerance is the willingness of buyers and participants to accept more formal rules, steps, and structure in exchange for legitimacy, traceability, and decision quality.

## Business-model implication

Decidim also clarifies a different tradeoff.

Open infrastructure can still require:

- implementation partners
- governance design
- tenant-specific configuration
- process architecture work

Public partner signals make that ecosystem visible.

The Decidim partners page currently distinguishes public institutions from service providers, and the service-provider side includes firms such as Pokecode, Octree, Colectic, Wildest Future, Code for Japan, Urban Equipe, Digidem Lab, and others.

The formal partner policy is also economically revealing.

Organizations that want to become Decidim Partners are expected to contribute 3 percent of the yearly income generated by Decidim activities, or 1.5 percent for nonprofit organizations.

That is a clear signal that Decidim expects a surrounding service economy to exist and to generate revenue from implementation, management of instances, and related activities.

That matters because the economic capture is not concentrated only in a single proprietary vendor.

Part of the value sits in the open core, and part sits in the surrounding implementation and service ecosystem.

So Decidim clarifies a different architecture-versus-service pattern:

- the platform itself is infrastructure
- a meaningful share of the economics is pushed outward to integrators, specialist partners, and process designers

So Decidim is another reminder that infrastructure products do not eliminate integration work.

They justify it when the resulting system becomes dependable enough.

The 2026 Open Source Politics acquisition discussion adds another useful signal.

Decidim explicitly described Open Source Politics as one of its early international partners and, after the acquisition by Go Vocal, publicly emphasized that there were multiple service-provider options available to maintain client platforms.

That is important because it confirms that the practical continuity of Decidim deployments often depends on the surrounding partner ecosystem rather than on a single vertically integrated vendor.

That is highly relevant for DMG.

It suggests the right comparison is not between:

- zero-friction product
- heavy product

The more relevant comparison is between:

- systems that require architecture work but generate dependable structured outputs
- systems that stay lightweight upfront but repeatedly pay the cost of messy downstream interpretation

## Procurement and maintenance clue

The Lons-le-Saunier deployment gives a useful small-city procurement clue for the Decidim stack.

According to local reporting, the city worked with Open Source Politics to adapt Decidim to the municipality's needs for 14,700 euros in the first year and 3,000 euros in the following years.

That split is strategically useful because it makes two things visible at once:

- there is an upfront adaptation and setup cost tied to local requirements
- there is a lower recurring maintenance cost once the platform is in place

This is not enough to generalize full Decidim economics.

But it is strong evidence that even open democratic infrastructure can still follow a practical model of initial integration plus recurring upkeep.

That matters for DMG because it supports the idea that architecture-heavy systems are still buyable when the buyer believes the process value is durable enough.

## French continuity and sovereignty signal

France now also provides a useful warning about the service layer around open democratic infrastructure.

The French Senate petitions platform and the French National Assembly petitions platform both publicly identify Open Source Politics as the builder and Decidim as the software base.

That confirms two things at once:

- Decidim is live inside serious French institutional settings
- a meaningful share of practical delivery sat with a French specialist service provider rather than with Decidim alone

The April 2026 Decidim Association statement adds the harder part.

It says Open Source Politics was acquired by Go Vocal after financial difficulties over several years, and it explicitly tells OSP clients that other Decidim service providers are available to help maintain their platforms during the transition.

That is strategically revealing.

It shows that the open-software story does not remove operational dependency.

It just moves more of the dependency into the surrounding service ecosystem.

That is a very useful lesson for DMG.

The real comparison is not between dependency and no dependency.

It is between different dependency shapes:

- dependence on one closed vendor with packaged software and support
- dependence on an open core plus a service-provider ecosystem whose continuity can still become fragile

## Financial-status note

I did not find a simple public company-style valuation framing for Decidim during this pass, because the public identity is more institutional and association-based than startup-financial in the usual SaaS sense.

The stronger public signals here are governance and ecosystem signals rather than venture-style funding optics.

That itself is strategically relevant.

Decidim can be read less as a venture-scaled engagement vendor and more as democratic infrastructure with an implementation and community ecosystem around it.
