## What IMAGO appears to be

IMAGO is not a narrow form-fill product.

Publicly, it presents as a broad medico-social DUI / operating suite covering:

- handicap / PH
- protection de l’enfance / PDE
- personne en difficulté spécifique / PDS
- accueil, hébergement, insertion / AHI
- grand âge / EHPAD
- RH and planning
- management and reporting through associated modules such as Gessi and Analytics

The public pages also emphasize:

- Ségur reference coverage across multiple domains
- mobile access on tablet, smartphone, and desktop
- continuity through Imago Duplicata
- HDS and security posture
- web-first ergonomics
- training and support around the ecosystem

The product language is explicit about breadth: one suite, multiple sector variants, and a governance/pilotage story that already includes reporting and statistics.

## The public claim that matters most

One line is strategically important because it constrains DMG positioning:

- IMAGO says information is entered once and then distributed where it needs to be

That means a generic pitch about making data easier to structure, easier to distribute, or more visible is not enough.

IMAGO already claims that terrain.

DMG would need a narrower and more specific gap.

## What the call actually surfaced

The call did not point to a vague “data problem” in the abstract.

It surfaced a more local operational difficulty in a medico-social setting:

- ease of data entry matters
- managers need data they can actually exploit
- staff need the organization to create discipline around structured entry
- a tariffing / transition problem may create repeated mapping work in a specific sector context

That is useful, but it should stay calibrated as a sector-specific operational issue rather than being inflated into a universal product thesis.

Eric’s request was also tactically important:

- he asked for a review of IMAGO
- he asked whether DMG could potentially be greffed on top of it
- he was open to network intros and even budget conversations if the problem proved real enough

The correct interpretation is that the next step is exploration and workload assessment, not immediate product proof.

## Strategic read for DMG

DMG should not be framed as a replacement for IMAGO.

That would conflict with IMAGO’s public coverage and with the way the sector already buys DUI systems.

The more credible hypothesis is narrower:

- DMG may be able to act as a non-invasive layer around an existing DUI
- the layer would need to add decision legibility, structured input, or cross-system signal where the underlying DUI does not already solve the need
- the value would come from governance logic and integration fit, not from replacing the core record system

This is aligned with the broader DMG strategy notes on [non-invasive support and integration fit](../../../integrated-support-as-moat.md) and on [DMG vs post-hoc signal extraction](../../../dmg-vs-post-hoc-signal-extraction.md).

It is also aligned with the more general rule in [decision support boundaries](../../../decision-support-boundaries.md): structuring inputs, synthesizing them, and owning final decision authority are not the same layer.

## What DMG is not, in this comparison

DMG should not be pitched here as:

- a better DUI
- a direct competitor to IMAGO’s whole suite
- a generic dashboard for medical-social data
- a survey or feedback tool
- a promise that one small integration will magically solve a large software replacement problem

That would be too broad, and too close to what IMAGO already says it handles.

## Where DMG could still fit

If there is a fit, it is most likely one of these three shapes:

1. A structured decision layer sitting beside IMAGO, not replacing it.
2. A small integration or workflow extension that makes a specific recurring decision problem easier to inspect.
3. A proof-of-capability slice that shows DMG can sit on top of an existing sector stack and create better signal without forcing rip-and-replace.

The third option is strategically attractive because it could help with both domain credibility and investor credibility.

But it still needs to be earned.

## Working conclusion

The strongest current conclusion is:

- IMAGO is broad enough that DMG should not try to compete as another DUI
- the only serious opening is a narrow non-invasive governance or decision-support layer
- the real validation task is to prove that the observed pain is repeated, expensive, and not already solved by IMAGO’s native surface
- if that proof exists, the result could be a strong integration-backed wedge and a credible proof-of-capability story

## Next questions to validate

- What exact workflow or decision problem remains unsolved after IMAGO’s own features are accounted for?
- Is the pain about data entry discipline, about manager-level exploitation of data, or about a very specific tariff/model transition?
- Does IMAGO expose enough integration surface for a non-invasive layer to sit on top without becoming a second disconnected tool?
- Would the opportunity justify the integration workload and support burden, or would it stay as an interesting but non-urgent adjacency?
