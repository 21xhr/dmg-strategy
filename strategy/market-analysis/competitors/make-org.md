# Make.org

**Snapshot date**: 2026-04-16

## Why this file matters

Make.org is worth deeper analysis because it appears to be more than a generic civic-participation product.

It looks relevant both as:

- a civic-participation and mass-consultation platform
- a signal that institutions, NGOs, and private organizations already pay for structured consultation and decision-support tooling
- a strong example of a product thesis built around extracting usable signal from large volumes of messy expression

## Current thesis

The strongest current read is that Make.org is not mainly a governance-by-rules system.

It is closer to a consultation-and-consensus-extraction company with a civic-tech wedge.

Publicly, it presents itself as a neutral and independent civic-tech organization.

Operationally, its public offer is closer to:

- deploy a participation environment for a client or public cause
- collect proposals, votes, comments, and recommendations at scale
- use moderation, sociological analysis, data science, and some AI-assisted processing to identify what is consensual, controversial, or actionable
- convert that output into reports, priority maps, and action plans

That makes Make.org relevant to DMG not because the products are identical, but because it validates real buyer demand for tools that reduce the gap between institutional decision-making and large-scale expressed demand.

## Current verified public signals

From Make.org public-facing material, the `about.make.org` site, and public company registries, there are already visible signals that the company operates at meaningful consultation scale and sells real projects rather than only publishing ideas.

Examples visible in public pages include:

- large consultation narratives around social causes and public-interest topics
- references to action plans following consultations
- public examples with tens of thousands or more participants
- positioning around citizen expression, consultation, and concrete follow-up
- an explicit project page aimed at public institutions, private organizations, and NGOs
- public company data showing a real operating business, not only a symbolic civic initiative

Examples seen during this first pass include:

- 130,061 participants, 2,278 proposals, and more than 305,890 votes for the Diversity & Inclusion consultation
- 50,733 participants, 2,105 proposals, and 269,838 votes for the urban-life consultation
- 13,946 participants, 360 proposals, and 107,271 votes for the Swiss youth service consultation
- public buyer and partner signals such as Banque des Territoires, CESE, CNDP, the European Committee of the Regions, and various NGOs and institutions

These numbers matter because they show Make.org is not only a small niche participation tool.

## What Make.org appears to sell publicly

The clearest public offer appears on the project page and splits into two participation products plus an adjacent AI layer.

### Consultation platform

- one open question
- participant proposals and votes
- human-driven moderation
- algorithmic and human analysis to identify consensual and controversial ideas
- reports that define actionable priorities

### Dialogue platform

- present initiatives in a customized interface
- collect comments and recommendations
- use AI-powered analysis plus human moderation
- identify the measures generating the most engagement and the clearest implementation path
- support collaborative strategy and action-plan building

### Delivery model

The public offer is not only software.

It includes:

- platform setup and fine-tuning
- hosting, maintenance, security, and anti-trolling measures
- support for communication and mobilisation
- analysis and publication of results

This looks closer to consultation-as-a-service plus platform than to a pure self-serve analytics product.

### Buyer types publicly named

The public project page explicitly addresses:

- public institutions
- private organizations
- NGOs

That matters because it confirms the non-civic-commercial layer is not just a guess.

## How much structure Make.org designs upstream

Make.org does structure interaction upstream to a meaningful extent.

Its public offer is not just passive observation of chaos.

It designs consultation environments with:

- a defined question or project frame
- a limited set of interaction types such as proposals, votes, comments, and recommendations
- moderation and anti-trolling measures
- customized platform setup for the client or cause

So the sharper distinction is not:

- Make.org does no structuring
- DMG does structuring

The sharper distinction is:

- Make.org structures a consultation device so broad participation becomes legible enough to analyze afterward
- DMG aims to structure the governance rules of the interaction itself so the resulting behavior is economically meaningful before the analysis layer even begins

That is a real difference in depth.

Make.org is still closer to structured consultation plus post-hoc synthesis.

DMG is closer to rule-designed arbitration under scarcity.

## Why Make.org can be considered a competitor or adjacent competitor

Make.org is relevant when the buyer need is:

- collect large volumes of proposals or opinions
- identify major themes at scale
- align action plans with a broad base of participants
- reduce the gap between leadership decisions and what a population expresses

That overlaps with DMG on:

- structured consultation
- demand visibility
- uncertainty reduction
- decision support under noisy conditions

It is especially relevant as an adjacent competitor when the buyer wants to observe and synthesize what a large group wants before deciding how to act.

## Why Make.org is still not the same product as DMG

Even if the overlap is real, the models are not identical.

Make.org appears closer to:

- mass consultation
- consensus emergence
- large-scale proposal collection and analysis
- action-plan alignment after broad participation
- extracting decision-usable signal after interaction has already happened

DMG is currently closer to:

- operational arbitration
- scarce influence allocation
- prioritization under constrained execution capacity
- governance inside recurring day-to-day workflows
- designing interaction rules so the behavior itself is more legible upstream

This is the sharper hypothesis difference:

- Make.org: if enough people express themselves and the analysis layer is strong enough, signal can be extracted from noise.
- DMG: when the interaction itself is chaotic, the stronger move is to structure the interaction rules so meaningful behavior is generated before analysis.

So the competitive lesson is not that Make.org and DMG are identical.

It is that Make.org is a strong signal that organizations already buy products in the broader zone between consultation, alignment, and decision support, but it also clarifies what DMG is not.

DMG is not only another consensus-extraction layer.

Its stronger claim is that behavior can be designed through system rules, which changes the quality of the generated data rather than only improving the interpretation of noisy output.

## What data Make.org seems to use

The public evidence currently points to a fairly specific data model.

The core raw material seems to be generated inside Make.org-run participation environments, not gathered mainly from ambient social-media scraping.

Publicly described inputs include:

- participant proposals
- votes on proposals
- comments
- recommendations
- debate materials and contextual content attached to the consultation flow

Publicly described outputs include:

- consensual ideas
- controversial ideas
- grouped thematic axes
- reports and action plans

So the practical logic appears to be:

- deploy a client- or cause-specific consultation device
- collect large volumes of messy but structured-enough in-platform interaction
- filter and cluster that interaction into priorities that can be handed back to decision-makers

That is important because it means the analysis engine is mostly operating on data generated by their own interaction environment.

## Panoramic AI

Panoramic matters, but it should not be confused with the core consultation engine.

From public material, Panoramic AI appears to be a separate AI layer created in 2024 that:

- answers open questions from a curated base of verified public documents
- summarizes complex debates and events
- gives access to source excerpts, documents, videos, and transcripts
- is aimed at citizens, journalists, civil society organizations, and public institutions

Public use cases mentioned include:

- the Citizens' Convention on End-of-Life
- EuroPCom 2024 with the European Committee of the Regions
- Wahlcheck 2025 in Germany
- the CNDP nuclear debate

This matters strategically because Panoramic reinforces Make.org's position as an information-access and synthesis company, not only as a participation interface.

But from the currently visible evidence, Panoramic is not the same thing as extracting consensus from participant-generated votes and proposals.

It is closer to an AI-powered access layer over verified document sets and event materials.

## Likely cost structure versus DMG

Public material suggests a heavier service layer than DMG's current product direction.

Make.org publicly describes:

- platform setup and customization
- hosting and security management
- communication and mobilisation support
- moderation
- sociologists and data scientists analysing outputs
- AI-assisted processing layered on top

That likely makes the operating model more service-heavy and more expensive to deliver than a system whose main leverage comes from simpler recurring rules and a lighter analysis burden.

This should still be framed as an inference, not a verified pricing claim.

I did not find public price sheets proving Make.org is more expensive.

But the delivery model strongly suggests higher human-service costs and lower marginal simplicity than DMG if DMG can keep its promise of getting better data by structuring behavior earlier.

Lower marginal simplicity means that each additional client, project, or consultation likely requires more custom setup, moderation, analysis, or human work, so it does not scale as cleanly or cheaply as a more standardized product.

## Why this matters for DMG market research

This is exactly why deeper market research matters.

At first glance, Make.org can look like a civic-participation example.

On closer inspection, it may also validate demand for:

- hosted participation environments for institutions and organizations
- strategic alignment tools built on consultation and synthesis

That makes it a serious case study for the uncertainty-reduction pillar, not only for the civic vertical.

It also sharpens the contrast with DMG:

- Make.org improves interpretation of broad collective expression.
- DMG should argue for better design of the expression process itself when the real problem is arbitration under scarcity.

There are also public signals of past capital increases and later capital reduction, plus public innovation-aid entries visible on Pappers.

### Public funding and capital-history clues

Accessible public capital-history traces currently suggest the following sequence:

- 2016 creation with 1,000 euros of capital
- late 2016 increase to 381,000 euros
- mid 2017 increase to 529,952 euros
- late 2017 increase to 859,532 euros
- 2019 increase to 1,056,017 euros
- 2023 reduction by 739,211.90 euros down to 316,805.10 euros, reflected in 2024 registry notices

Public linked-entity traces on Pappers also point to possible financing or cap-table clues involving:

- Ingage in 2016
- Invess Ile-de-France in 2019
- TS Holding SAS in 2019
- Oneragtime Make.org II SAS in 2022

These are useful clues, but they should not be overstated.

Because beneficial-owner access is restricted, the safe claim is only that public filings and linked-entity traces suggest outside financing and cap-table evolution over time.

There is also a visible public aid signal on Pappers tied to the 2024-2026 RDI regime, including:

- a 4,204,920 euro research-industrial aid entry
- a 200,000 euro recoverable-advance style entry dated September 2024

Those signals matter because they suggest Make.org is not funded only through commercial revenue.

Its capital history and public aid traces indicate a mixed financing reality: operating revenue, institutional contracts, capital events, and innovation support.

### Valuation status

Valuation appears important, but I did not find a trustworthy free public valuation number in accessible sources.

There are public profile pages on Crunchbase, PitchBook, and similar databases, but the accessible snippets do not expose a verifiable valuation figure.

So for now, the best hard public business signals are:

- revenue scale
- headcount band
- capital history
- public procurement and institutional client signals
- public aid and financing traces where visible

That is enough to confirm that Make.org is a real operating company with meaningful commercial and institutional activity, even if the valuation remains unresolved.
