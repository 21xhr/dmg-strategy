# Participation And Adoption

## Purpose

This document extracts the durable product-market-fit and participation ideas from the brainstorm source.

## Core adoption rule

If participation feels cognitively expensive, adoption collapses.

DMG therefore needs to make a strong mechanism feel easy to use.

The product should feel simpler than the underlying logic.

## Zero-friction participation

The system should not require users to understand formal governance theory.

What matters is that they can act quickly while still producing a better signal.

That means:

- simple contribution surfaces
- obvious tradeoffs
- low training cost
- visible consequences of influence allocation

## Delegation and specialization

One promising adoption idea is delegated or specialized influence.

Examples:

- a user delegates influence on a topic to a trusted participant
- a user specializes on one theme

Why this matters:

- it lowers cognitive load
- it creates identity and role formation inside the system
- it can make a complex influence economy feel socially intuitive

This is close to a liquid-democracy style pattern, but productized for operational environments.

## Thematic governance

The system becomes easier to use when participation is scoped.

An operator can open one theme, domain, or decision class.

That helps both sides:

- the operator keeps control of the decision surface
- participants know what kind of contribution is useful right now

## Why this matters commercially

Better participation design improves:

- conversion
- retention
- quality of signal
- perceived fairness

It also strengthens the thesis that DMG is useful outside streaming.

The same low-friction rule matters in:

- SaaS product governance
- service-business prioritization
- internal organization workflows
- institutional participation surfaces

## Practical Product Market Fit (PMF) questions to keep pressure-testing

1. Why would a participant come back the next day?
	Answer: a sense of agency drives return. Users come back because they feel they have a real lever on the decision process.
2. What makes the effort feel worthwhile?
	Answer: value is realized when participation has visible consequences. For example, the effort feels justified the moment an idea moves up the priority stack, giving the user an immediate proof of impact that validates the time spent. That is only one of the ways DMG can make participation visibly consequential.
3. Should influence be earned, purchased, or both?
	Answer: whether money can buy influence is context-dependent. In some settings, especially educational or pure civic settings, purchased influence may be inappropriate or forbidden. In commercial or service settings, financial commitment such as business volume or deposits may legitimately contribute to influence if that matches the tenant's rules.
4. How does the system stay usable under pressure?
	Answer: it should self-regulate as much as possible through the risk of losing influence or reputation, with the decision-maker retaining final authority to exclude malicious actors.

## Tenant philosophy

DMG is a governance infrastructure that should adapt to the philosophy of the tenant.

A product like this should not impose one universal moral model.

It should provide the sliders, guardrails, and rule surfaces that let the operator define the rules of the game for that environment.

That matters because the acceptable balance between earned influence, purchased influence, moderation strictness, and reputation loss is not identical across streaming, SaaS, service businesses, internal organizations, or institutional settings.

## Uncertainty reduction angle

Another strong PMF angle is uncertainty reduction.

See [[uncertainty-reduction]].

The short version is that DMG becomes valuable when it compresses feedback lag and turns ambiguous demand into a more immediate, structured signal for action.

## Useful short framing

The current web measures noise cheaply.

DMG should make deliberate participation feel almost as easy while producing a much better decision signal.
