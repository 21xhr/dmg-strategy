# Service Company Wedge

## Purpose

This note captures a practical wedge for DMG in service-company environments.

The goal is to clarify what the product would actually look like outside streaming, how it could integrate into existing systems, and why that integration may increase the value of the product rather than weaken it.

## Core thesis

For a service company, DMG should not be framed first as a broad collaboration suite.

The stronger framing is a governance layer that makes upstream participation more decision-grade.

In plain terms:

- the company already receives requests, preferences, complaints, and suggestions
- not all of that input is equally useful for prioritization
- the company already has real constraints such as time, staff capacity, contract commitments, and strategic focus
- DMG can make influence legible under those constraints instead of letting loudness or randomness dominate

## Make better behavior rational and legible

Making better behavior rational means designing the system so that participants have a reason to contribute in ways that are more useful to the tenant.

Making behavior legible means that the resulting input is easier to interpret without excessive downstream guesswork.

That can happen when the system:

- makes tradeoffs explicit
- attaches visible rules to influence
- rewards clearer and more structured participation
- records not only preference but the conditions under which that preference was expressed

The point is not to eliminate chaos completely.

It is to make more meaningful and actionable participation easier to generate and easier to read.

## Likely product shape

For a service company, the product likely has three layers.

### Participant-facing layer

This is the surface where clients, users, partners, or stakeholders can:

- express support
- allocate influence
- prioritize requests
- participate under explicit rules

This might look like:

- an embedded module inside an existing product or account surface
- a workflow extension inside tools the tenant already uses
- a dedicated portal where stakeholders return to track priorities and spend influence over time

Concrete examples could include:

- a priority-request widget inside a SaaS account area
- a structured prioritization module attached to support, roadmap, or service requests
- a client or partner portal showing open priorities, rules of influence, and visible tradeoffs

### Tenant-facing governance and insight layer

This is where the company:

- configures influence rules
- defines categories, constraints, and weighting logic
- sees the resulting signal
- audits why some requests or demands carried more weight than others

This is not just a dashboard.

It is the operating interface for the rule system.

### Integration layer

This is where DMG connects to what the company already uses.

Examples could include:

- CRM data
- contract or subscription tiers
- usage history
- support and ticketing tools
- delivery workflows
- category tags, account status, or historical engagement signals

This is where a large part of the value can be created.

## Why integration may be a strength rather than a weakness

At first glance, integration can look like a sales problem because it implies setup work.

But serious buyers already pay for integration when they believe it produces more dependable downstream processing.

That is why the ERP analogy matters.

If the integration work makes the system materially better at reading and governing participation, then it is not only implementation drag.

It is part of the moat.

Once DMG is connected to real operational data and real decision flows:

- the signal becomes more tenant-specific
- the system becomes harder to replace casually
- switching away can mean losing accumulated rule design, process fit, and data interpretation logic

That does not mean lock-in should be the sales pitch.

It means that real operational fit can create retention and defensibility after deployment.

## Why this is not just another feedback SaaS

Feedback tools usually help collect and summarize what customers say they want.

DMG should be framed differently.

It helps determine:

- whose input carries which weight
- under what rules influence is earned or spent
- how tradeoffs are surfaced before decisions are made
- how requests become more actionable before they enter downstream prioritization

So the relevant promise is not only better collection.

It is better governed input.

## Contact-probing questions

This is the right kind of wedge to test in contact conversations.

Good questions include:

- where do too many requests currently compete for limited execution capacity
- which customer or stakeholder inputs are currently overrepresented because they are louder, faster, or easier to surface
- which participants should sometimes carry more influence, and why
- what existing systems already contain signals that could be used to weight participation more intelligently
- where does the organization already spend time translating messy demand into something operationally actionable
- where would clearer participation rules reduce repeated interpretation work or disputes

These questions help identify whether the buyer has a real decision-governance problem rather than only a generic engagement problem.

## Wedge summary

The strongest service-company story is not:

- here is another place where your clients can vote

The stronger story is:

- here is a governance layer that helps you structure influence, generate more decision-usable input, and connect participation to the operational systems that already run your business
