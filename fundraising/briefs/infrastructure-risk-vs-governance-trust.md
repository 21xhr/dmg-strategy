# Infrastructure Risk vs Governance Trust

## Purpose

Investor-facing note on why trust and continuity matter once participation software starts to behave like infrastructure.

Keep sequencing and founder-credibility detail in [[fundraising/briefs/investor-sequencing-note]] and [[fundraising/briefs/founder-risk-memo]].

## Core point

Once participation software becomes real infrastructure, the question is no longer only whether the interface is good.

The harder question is whether the institution can trust the system, the operator behind it, and the continuity layer around it.

## Why this matters

French Decidim deployments make the issue visible.

The petition platforms of the French Senate and the French National Assembly publicly identify Open Source Politics as the implementation partner and Decidim as the software base.

That proves democratic infrastructure can become institutionally important while still depending on a relatively small implementation ecosystem.

The 2026 acquisition of Open Source Politics by Go Vocal after financial difficulties makes the continuity problem concrete.

Even if the software layer is strong, the service layer can still become fragile.

The continuity layer is the practical capacity to keep the deployment working over time: implementation support, maintenance ownership, client transition handling, and operational accountability when something changes.

Practically, it answers who keeps this working and who catches the fall if something changes.

That matters because infrastructure gets judged on continuity, not only on features.

## Investor takeaway

The useful comparison is not only open versus closed.

It is also:

- packaged vendor software with integrated support and stronger continuity control
- open or modular infrastructure that still depends on a fragile implementation ecosystem

That matters for DMG because it suggests the product should not be imagined as software alone.

If DMG wants to become governance infrastructure beyond the streaming wedge, it likely needs an integrated support and integration layer credible enough to make institutions trust the full stack, not just the interface.

That does not require DMG to behave like a destructive rip-and-replace vendor.

One of the stronger product framings is the opposite: a platform-agnostic, non-invasive governance layer that can plug into existing infrastructure, read the signals already present in the tenant stack, and strengthen or add governance surfaces where current ones are weak, absent, or too generic.

That makes the offer easier to trust because the buyer does not need to rebuild everything to get value.

## Why this can be valuable

That makes the project more complex, but it can also make it more defensible.

If DMG owns more of the rule design, integration, and continuity layer, then more of the value capture sits inside the company rather than leaking into a fragile partner ecosystem.

## Real investor concern

The likely investor concern is not that the problem is too small.

It is whether the founder and team can operate a product that starts to look less like a simple software feature and more like a governance-infrastructure company.

That is a scale-of-execution question, not a reason the thesis is weak.
