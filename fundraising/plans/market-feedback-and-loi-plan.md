## Why this matters now

At the current stage, the immediate goal is not to close a full sales process.

It is to collect three kinds of signal:

- problem validation
- pilot willingness
- early commercial seriousness

This is useful because it helps:

- choose the strongest wedge
- improve the pitch with real operator language
- identify which verticals react with real urgency rather than polite interest
- produce credible pre-revenue evidence for investors or advisors

## Suggested outreach sequence

### Stage 1. Discovery and language testing

Goal:

- verify the pain in the contact's own words
- avoid pitching features before the problem is confirmed

Use discovery to confirm problem reality, not to do generic relationship-building.

What to capture:

- where decisions are currently made with poor signal
- what feedback lag looks like in practice
- whether the pain is prioritization, legitimacy, uncertainty, or all three
- what the current workaround is

Across larger organizations, probe systematically for organizational feedback lag and structured internal demand, not only explicit prioritization pain.

For groups such as STL Nautisme and Groupe LB, this should include internal usage questions such as:

- where management acts top-down with weak employee feedback
- where communication failures distort operational priorities
- where ERP or process friction hides the real pain
- whether growth has increased internal coordination and visibility problems

### Stage 2. Tailored product reaction

Goal:

- show the same engine through the contact's context
- test whether the use case feels credible and useful

Output:

- short follow-up memo or demo framing tailored to that vertical

### Stage 3. Pilot interest qualification

Goal:

- determine whether the person would seriously test, sponsor, or introduce the product

Questions:

- would this be worth piloting if the product reached a minimally usable state for your context
- what would need to be true for you to test it
- who else would need to approve or participate
- what rough budget or value band would make sense if the pilot worked

### Stage 4. Non-binding commercial signal

Possible outputs:

- a written email confirming the pain and interest
- a short pilot-intent note
- a non-binding letter of intent
- a referral to a better contact in the same vertical

## What a letter of intent is at this stage

At a pre-revenue or pre-seed stage, an LOI is usually a non-binding statement that a real organization would be interested in piloting, evaluating, or potentially buying the product if certain conditions are met.

It is not a contract.

It is not revenue.

It is not proof of product-market fit by itself.

## Why an LOI can still be valuable

Used honestly, it can show:

- the pain is real enough that someone is willing to put their name behind interest
- the use case is specific rather than hypothetical
- a buyer can imagine budget, pilot scope, or operational value
- the founder is not only pitching abstractions

## Best LOI shape for DMG right now

The strongest early version is simple and specific.

It should say:

- who the organization is
- what problem they recognize
- what DMG would be used for in their context
- what kind of pilot or evaluation they would consider
- what conditions would need to be met first
- optionally, a rough budget range or value expectation

## Important caution

Do not overclaim LOIs.

One CEO saying they could imagine spending money on this is useful signal.

It is not the same thing as committed revenue.

What matters most is the combination of:

- role seniority
- specificity of the use case
- seriousness of the written interest
- repeatability across multiple contacts

## Better-than-LOI outcomes in some cases

In some contexts, these may be equally or more useful than a formal LOI:

- an introduction to the real operational owner when the first contact is senior but not the hands-on buyer
- agreement to run a structured pilot conversation
- willingness to review a tailored workflow mockup
- a clear statement of current pain, likely spending rationale, and buying conditions

What a structured pilot conversation means:

- one explicit conversation focused on use case, workflow, stakeholders, pilot scope, success criteria, blockers, and decision path
- not a vague "interesting product" discussion
- not yet a commercial negotiation

When a tailored workflow mockup is worth doing:

- when the contact already recognizes the pain
- when one adapted flow would help them picture DMG in their own context
- when the mockup can stay lightweight, such as one short memo, one diagram, or one or two adapted interface screens

Often the right order is:

- first understand the operator's current pain in their own words
- then reflect that pain back clearly
- only then decide whether a tailored mockup is worth the effort

This should not mean building a custom product branch too early.

For streamers in particular, a pilot-intent note or willingness-to-test statement is often more realistic than a formal LOI.

## Working rule

The immediate objective is not to collect as many names as possible.

It is to collect a small number of serious, specific signals from credible operators across a few distinct verticals.
